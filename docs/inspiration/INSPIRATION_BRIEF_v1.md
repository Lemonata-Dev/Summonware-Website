# Inspiration Brief — v1
**For:** Summonware (software house — custom solutions/products across business needs, AI-forward, modern tech stack)
**Built from:** structural assessment of 5 reference sites (neko.engineering, wembi.ai, cmblu.com, agentura.framer.website, cipherdigital.com)
**Status:** proposed — not yet approved as canonical

---

## 1. The tone decision

None of the 5 references are the right register on their own:

| Site | Why not a direct fit |
|---|---|
| Neko | Consumer-gadget playfulness — too "toy," undercuts B2B software credibility |
| Agentura | Fashion/editorial, zero color, no product proof — reads as an art portfolio, not a software vendor |
| Wembi | Right technical depth, but the manifesto/poetic Italian-editorial voice is too abstract for "we build working software for your business" |
| Cmblu | Right credibility level, but very industrial/hardware-coded (batteries, factories) — needs translating to a digital-native register |
| Cipher | Closest in *restraint and institutional trust*, but almost too static/finance-facing for a company that wants to also feel current and AI-forward |

**Chosen tone:** *Confident technical clarity with quiet momentum.* Summonware should feel like the calm, competent partner who ships — not the flashiest booth at the trade show, not a cold enterprise PDF either. Structural seriousness (Cipher, Cmblu) + a restrained version of numbered/systemic storytelling (Wembi) + one deliberate signature motion moment (borrowed in spirit, not literally, from Neko's scroll-driven feature sequence) — built at a scale a real software house's marketing site should be, not a hardware launch page.

---

## 2. What we're taking, from where, and why

**From Cipher Digital — the connective visual system (adapted)**
Cipher's single reused grid/line system (one visual device doing three jobs: framing, backdrop, data table) is the most *buildable* and *scalable* pattern of the five — critical since Summonware's site needs to grow with new sections without inventing a new motif every time. We adopt this as the site's core connective device, but themed to Summonware's own colors/tokens, not copied pixel-for-pixel.

**From Wembi — numbered section rhythm + progressive text reveal**
The "N.001 / SECTION NAME / N.001" divider header is a clean, low-cost way to give a growing multi-section page a sense of structure and pace, and doubles as a natural anchor for future added sections (append N.007, N.008…). The word-by-word scroll-linked paragraph reveal is a tasteful, cheap-to-build motion signature — more appropriate for a software company's "how it works" copy than heavy 3D.

**From Cmblu — the alternating proof-strip pattern + hexagon-link CTA**
Cmblu's repeating "icon-arrow + label" link (used for all secondary CTAs) is a clean, reusable component — good for a site that will keep adding case studies/blog posts over time. The mixed-weight paragraph (bold lead-in, gray continuation) is an easy, elegant way to open a credibility section without a wall of even-weight text.

**From Neko — one signature interactive moment, not the whole language**
Rather than adopting Neko's fully scroll-scrubbed page, we take just the *idea*: one flagship section ("How Summonware builds" or "Our process") gets a numbered step sequence where a visual (product UI mockup / workflow diagram) updates as you scroll through steps. This becomes Summonware's one "wow" moment, not the whole site's operating principle — keeping build cost and Fable-5-low-effort feasibility in check.

**From Agentura — explicitly not taken**
Corner-pinned micro-copy and monochrome fashion-editorial imagery don't serve a software company that needs to show real product screens, diagrams, and client logos. We leave this pattern out entirely; noted here only so future versions don't accidentally reintroduce it without a deliberate reason.

## 3. Structural flow for Summonware's homepage

1. **Hero** — clear statement of what Summonware does (not abstract wordplay): headline + one-line proof point + primary CTA. Calm background: subtle abstract grid/motion (Cipher-style), not full-bleed photography.
2. **Trust strip** — logos of clients/partners, sitting inside a smooth gradient transition (Cmblu-style), not a bordered box.
3. **Numbered section divider** ("N.01 — WHAT WE DO") introduces the services overview — reused for every major section from here down.
4. **Services/capabilities grid** — 3–4 cards, mixed-weight intro paragraph above it (Cmblu-style credibility copy), hexagon-or-simple-arrow link CTA per card (Cmblu-style).
5. **Signature process section** — the one flagship scroll-driven moment: numbered steps (01–04) with a workflow/product visual that updates per step (Neko-inspired, Cipher-restrained execution).
6. **Case studies / proof** — Cipher-style grid-docked images growing to full-bleed, stat callouts (metrics like projects delivered, industries served) using the same grid system as a data table.
7. **Client testimonial** — single quote, plain, no gimmicks.
8. **Closing CTA** — direct, human (name + role, not a faceless form), single strong button — following Agentura's "make it personal" instinct without adopting its visual language.
9. **Footer** — standard, calm, information-dense (nav, legal, socials).

This flow is intentionally modular: each numbered section is self-contained, so adding a new service or case study later means duplicating an existing section pattern, not inventing new structure — directly serving your requirement #2 (easy future content additions).

---

## 4. Motion language for Summonware

- **Primary:** scroll-position-driven reveals (fade/slide-in), used sparingly and consistently — not scroll-scrubbing everywhere.
- **Signature moment:** one pinned numbered-step sequence (section 5 above) — the site's single "flagship" interaction, budgeted deliberately so it doesn't sprawl into every section.
- **Micro:** word-by-word paragraph reveal (Wembi-style) reserved for 1–2 key statements, not all body copy — overuse would slow the page down and feel gimmicky.
- **Explicitly avoided:** full-bleed blur-to-focus hero, dashed radial "energy" motifs, marker-highlight text boxes, corner-pinned micro-copy — all felt too tied to their original brand's identity to reuse safely.
